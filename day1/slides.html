The big idea, in one slide

An LLM turns text into numbers,
then numbers into predictions.

The interesting question is what the function predicts and how it produces the answer.

Day 1 · Movement 0105 / 30
Step 01 · Text becomes geometry

Words live in a high-dimensional space.
Similar meanings sit close together.

Modern models do not store words as letters. Each token is stored as a list of about a thousand numbers. That list is a position in a "meaning space" learned from billions of training sentences.

The geometry is what makes everything else possible. Once "Watch List" and "violation" sit near each other, every downstream task that uses those numbers gets a head start.

Think of it as a structured replacement for bag-of-words and TF-IDF, where the structure is meaning rather than co-occurrence.

Day 1 · Movement 0106 / 30
Step 02 · The split

Same starting geometry,
two very different jobs.

Family 01

Encoder models

Read a piece of text in full and output a fixed prediction. A label, a score, a probability.

BERT, RoBERTa, DeBERTa, ModernBERT. Smaller, faster, deterministic. Run on a laptop or a free Colab CPU.

CLASSIFY · SCORE · MEASURE
Family 02

Generative models

Continue text one token at a time. Given a prompt, produce more text. The prompt steers what comes out.

GPT, Claude, Gemini, LLaMA, Qwen. Larger, slower, stochastic. Usually called via an API.

EXTRACT · SUMMARIZE · GENERATE

Both families descend from the same Transformer architecture. They diverge in what they are trained to predict.

Day 1 · Movement 0107 / 30
Family 01 · The encoder

Read the sentence.
Return a number.

Use it for

  • Stance toward a target
  • Sentiment, tone, emotion
  • Topic and framing labels
  • Named entity recognition
  • Any fixed, comparable score per text

Why political scientists love it

Same input always produces the same output. Cheap on millions of paragraphs. Easy to validate against a hand-coded gold set.

Day 1 · Movement 0108 / 30
Family 02 · The generative model

Continue the text,
token by token.

Use it for

  • Structured extraction from messy text
  • Free-text summarization
  • Normalization of inconsistent fields
  • Reasoning over long context
  • Anything an encoder cannot express as a label

What you give up

Stochastic outputs by default. Higher cost per call. Harder to validate at scale. Reproducibility takes work.

Day 1 · Movement 0110 / 30
Three axes that decide for you

Cost. Speed. Reproducibility.

AxisEncoderGenerative
Cost / 1k texts ~$0 after model download. Local CPU is free. A few cents to a few dollars. Per token.
Speed ~50 to 500 texts per second on a free GPU. ~1 to 5 calls per second over the network.
Determinism Same input gives the same output every run. Stochastic by default. Pin temperature 0 and a fixed model version. Drift still happens.
Validation k-fold cross-validation. Confusion matrix. Kappa. Spot-check, schema validation, sample audits.
Replication Pin the model ID. Ship weights with your code. Pin the model version. Log every prompt and response.

Numbers are rough. Exact cost depends on text length, model size, and hardware tier.

Day 1 · Movement 0111 / 30
The trick that makes encoders flexible

Natural language inference.
The encoder's universal adapter.

An NLI-trained encoder learns one job. Given a premise and a hypothesis, decide whether the premise entails, contradicts, or is neutral with respect to the hypothesis.

That single skill is shockingly general. Any classification task can be rewritten as a yes-or-no entailment question.

Stance. "Does this passage support that the country has strong IP law?"

Topic. "Is this paragraph about agricultural trade?"

Framing. "Does this speech frame migration as a security issue?"

Day 1 · Movement 0112 / 30
Case study · Park, Aid, Lending, and TRIPS

Thirteen hypotheses,
one continuous score.

A paragraph from a US Trade Representative report becomes the premise. Each of 13 standardized claims becomes a hypothesis.

Each hypothesis carries a fixed weight from -2 to +2. The model returns 13 entailment probabilities. A weighted sum, scaled to -5 to +5, becomes the country-year IP-evaluation proxy.

The result. Pooled F1 of 0.87 across 13 hypotheses. Pearson correlation of 0.68 between encoder score and a hand-coded gold set on 300 paragraphs.

Park · Aid, Lending, and TRIPS. Code and data at github.com/jacqpark/nteText.

Day 1 · Movement 0113 / 30
Why an encoder, not a generative LLM
"
Stance detection is fundamentally a natural-language inference problem. DeBERTa's encoder architecture with NLI-specific pretraining yields higher accuracy and label efficiency in few-shot settings than generative models repurposed for classification through prompting.

Generative models also carry substantial computational overhead. DeBERTa's smaller footprint makes it feasible to fine-tune, validate through cross-validation, and deploy on standard academic hardware.

Park · Aid, Lending, and TRIPS · manuscript p. 14
Day 1 · Movement 0114 / 30
Movement 01 · Recap

Five things you now know.

01. The split

Two families. Encoder reads and labels. Generative continues text. Same Transformer, different training.

02. The default

If your task is a fixed label, start with an encoder. Cheaper, faster, easier to validate.

03. The escape hatch

Reach for a generative LLM when the answer cannot fit in a finite label set.

04. The NLI trick

An NLI-trained encoder is a universal classifier. Any task becomes a premise-hypothesis question with user-defined hypotheses.

05. The reproducibility tax

Encoders are deterministic with pinned weights. Generative models need extra discipline. Log prompts. Pin model versions. Audit samples.

microsoft/deberta-v3-large

  • ~435M parameters
  • 300 hand-labeled paragraphs

Four things to watch for
as the cells run.

01 · Easy wins

Factual markers

Paragraphs with explicit factual markers (Watch List, Priority Foreign Country) get classified correctly nearly every time.

02 · Interpretive misses

Where zero-shot stumbles

"The country has made progress" trips zero-shot models. Day 2 shows why fine-tuning fixes this.

03 · Aggregation

The mixed-paragraph problem

Most paragraphs contain both praise and criticism. A single label hides the mixture. Park's weighted-sum design is one solution.

04 · Validity

The question that follows you home

Off-the-shelf accuracy looks impressive in a demo. "Looks impressive" is far from "publishable." Validation closes the gap.

If something breaks

Four issues you will probably see,
30-second fixes for each.

SymptomCauseFix
RESTART REQUIRED warning after pip install. Colab pre-loaded packages were upgraded. Click Restart Runtime, then run cells from the top.
Model download stalls or fails. Hugging Face hub rate-limit or transient network. Wait 60 seconds, re-run the cell.
"CUDA out of memory" on GPU. Earlier cell loaded a too-large model. Runtime · Disconnect and delete runtime. Start fresh.
Cells run, output looks wrong. Edited labels but did not re-run upstream cells. Runtime · Run all. Re-runs in dependency order.

If none of these match, paste the last 6 lines of your error into the chat.
