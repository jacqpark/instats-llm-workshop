Day 1, Movement 0107 / 32
Step 01, Text becomes geometry

Words live in a high-dimensional space.
Similar meanings sit close together.

Modern models do not store words as letters. Each token is stored as a list of about a thousand numbers. That list is a position in a "meaning space" learned from billions of training sentences.

The geometry is what makes everything else possible. Once "Paris Agreement" and "carbon emissions" sit near each other, every downstream task that uses those numbers gets a head start.

Think of it as a structured replacement for bag-of-words and TF-IDF, where the structure is meaning rather than co-occurrence.

Day 1, Movement 0108 / 32
Step 02, The split

Same starting geometry.
Two very different jobs.

Family 01

Encoders

Read a piece of text in full and output a fixed prediction. A label, a score, a probability.

BERT · RoBERTa · DeBERTa · ModernBERT

Smaller, faster, deterministic. Run on a laptop or free Colab CPU.

DAY 1 + DAY 2
Family 02

Generative models

Continue text one token at a time. Given a prompt, produce more text. The prompt steers the output.

GPT · Claude · Gemini · LLaMA · Qwen

Larger, slower, stochastic. Usually called via API.

DAY 3

Both descend from the same Transformer architecture (2017). They diverge in what they were trained to predict. That difference shapes everything downstream.

Day 1, Movement 0109 / 32
Encoder deep dive

Read the whole sentence.
Return a number.

What kinds of numbers

  • Stance toward a target
  • Sentiment, tone, emotion
  • Topic and framing labels
  • Named entity tags
  • Anything where you need a fixed, comparable score per text

Why political scientists love them

  • Deterministic. Same input, same output. Run the same model in 2026 and 2030 and you get the same answer.
  • Cheap. After the model downloads once, inference is essentially free.
  • Validatable. The whole machinery of cross-validation and confusion matrices is right there.

For measurement projects, the default is encoder.

Day 1, Movement 0110 / 32
Generative deep dive

Continue the text,
token by token.

Use it for

  • Structured extraction from messy text
  • Free-text summarization
  • Normalization of inconsistent fields
  • Reasoning over long context
  • Anything where the answer cannot be a label from a finite list

What you give up

  • Stochastic by default. Same prompt, different answers. Pin temperature 0.
  • Higher cost per call. Tokens add up.
  • Reproducibility tax. Pin model versions. Log every prompt and response.

Reach for it when free-form output is the only fit. Day 3 is the deep dive.

Day 1, Movement 0112 / 32
Why encoders feel magical

The NLI trick.
One skill, every classification task.

An NLI-trained encoder learns one job. Given a premise and a hypothesis, decide whether the premise entails the hypothesis, contradicts it, or is neutral.

NLI stands for natural language inference. The DeBERTa we use today was pretrained on three NLI corpora that together contain millions of premise-hypothesis pairs.

That single skill is shockingly general. Any classification task can be rewritten as a yes-or-no entailment question with a clever choice of hypothesis.

Examples

  • Stance. "Does this passage support stronger climate action?"
  • Topic. "Is this paragraph about agricultural trade?"
  • Framing. "Does this speech frame migration as a security issue?"
zsc(text, candidate_labels=[
  "supports climate action",
  "skeptical of climate action",
  "does not discuss climate",
])
# returns one probability per label

A brief worked example

The same machinery,
scaled to a published proxy.

One quick example from my R&R paper at the Review of International Organizations.

Each paragraph from a US Trade Representative report is the premise. Thirteen claims about IP enforcement are the hypotheses. A weighted sum of the entailment probabilities becomes a country-year proxy for US assessments of foreign IP regimes, 1995 to 2022.

The same off-the-shelf NLI machinery you will run on UN speeches in fifteen minutes scales to a published measurement design with the right hypothesis engineering.

Day 117 / 32
Three things to know

Colab is a notebook,
plus a remote runtime,
plus a hardware menu.

Layer 01

The notebook

Cells of code and prose, like an Rmd file but interactive. Click a cell, press Shift Enter, output appears beneath it.

Layer 02

The remote runtime

The runtime is on Google's servers. Press run, the code executes there, you see the output in your browser.

Layer 03

The hardware menu

Pick CPU, GPU, or TPU. Free tier gives a CPU plus an occasional T4 GPU. Paid Colab Pro reliably attaches a beefier card.

For an encoder workflow, that GPU is roughly 10 to 50 times faster than CPU.

The dataset

The UN General Debate Corpus.
Eight thousand speeches, 1970 to present.

Specs

  • Source. Harvard Dataverse, doi:10.7910/DVN/0TJX8Y.
  • License. CC0. Fully open.
  • Coverage. Heads of delegation, opening of the UN General Assembly.
  • For today. The 2017-only snapshot mirrored in quanteda.corpora. About 196 speeches, 1 MB.
  • Sample. Sentence-tokenize, take roughly 200 sentences across countries.

Three sample sentences

"My country reaffirms its commitment to the Paris Agreement and calls on all states to raise their nationally determined contributions."

"Investment in fossil-fuel infrastructure remains a national priority for energy security and industrial development."

"The reform of the Security Council remains an urgent task that this Assembly must not defer for another decade."

The encoder will read each, score it, and give us back a stance label. Public corpus. Permissive license. Substantive question political scientists actually ask.

The model and the labels

One model.
Three plain-English labels.

The model

MoritzLaurer/DeBERTa-v3-base-mnli-fever-anli

  • License. MIT. Open weights.
  • Size. ~184 million parameters, ~735 MB.
  • Training. Three NLI corpora, millions of premise-hypothesis pairs.
  • Pipeline. zero-shot-classification.

Candidate labels

candidate_labels = [
  "the speaker supports stronger
   international climate action",
  "the speaker is skeptical of
   international climate action",
  "the speaker does not
   discuss climate",
]

We are not training. We are giving the model three plain-English statements and asking which one each sentence entails. The argmax of the three returned probabilities is the prediction.

Day 1, Movement 0325 / 32
Live, in the notebook

Three things to watch for.

Watch 01

Easy hits

Sentences that mention Paris Agreement, carbon emissions, or fossil fuels resolve cleanly to the supportive label, often above 0.85.

Sentences about peacekeeping or Security Council reform land in the off-topic bucket.

Watch 02

Hidden climate language

Sentences about "energy security" or "industrial transition" can carry a real climate stance the model misreads as off-topic.

Sentences about "rising seas" land correctly but at low confidence. The frame is implicit, not lexical.

Watch 03

Calibration

The model returns probabilities. Even confident-looking labels can be 0.55 versus 0.45 splits.

We will plot the probability histogram and discuss what cutoff a real paper would use.
