Day 205 / 30
Definition we are working toward

A measurement assigns numbers
to cases in a defensible way.

Property 01

Validity

The number tracks the concept it claims to track. Stance toward IP enforcement, not generic negativity.

Property 02

Reliability

Re-running the pipeline on the same input gives the same output. Two coders agree on the gold set.

Property 03

Comparability

Scores can be compared across countries and years without systematic drift introduced by the model.

Adcock and Collier (APSR 2001) is the canonical political-science treatment. Their framework predates LLMs but the demands are the same.

Day 2 · Movement 0106 / 30
Three threats to validity

What goes wrong
when you skip the validation step.

Threat 01

Concept slippage

The label "negative" captures sentiment, not stance. The model agrees with you but on the wrong concept.

Bestvater & Monroe (2023): the canonical example.

Threat 02

Mixed paragraphs

Real text contains both halves. The model picks the dominant signal. Israel 2017 looked positive because it opened with removal-from-list.

Threat 03

Subgroup drift

The model performs well on EU paragraphs and badly on small-economy paragraphs. Average accuracy hides this.

Each threat has a measurement remedy. Each remedy needs the same prerequisite.

Day 2 · Movement 0108 / 30
Sample size

Why thirty paragraphs
is the right number to start.

Why not five

Five paragraphs cannot distinguish a 60% accurate model from an 80% accurate one. The gold set has to be big enough to detect the differences you care about.

Why not three hundred

Three hundred paragraphs is a week of work. You will not actually do it before the workshop ends. The point is to get a real number on the board.

Thirty is

  • Enough to compute weighted F1 with a usable confidence interval.
  • Small enough that two coders can finish in an afternoon.
  • Big enough to spot subgroup drift if you stratify the sample.
  • The right scale for an iterative loop. Code thirty, refine the codebook, code thirty more if you need them.

Park's published proxy uses 300 hand-coded paragraphs under 10-fold cross-validation in the final validation. Thirty gets you to a defensible first cut. The rest is iteration.

Day 210 / 30
Step one

Write the codebook
before you touch the keyboard.

SCORE
Meaning
Decision rule
+1
US praises the country's IP regime.
Paragraph contains positive framing of enforcement, treaty accession, or removal from list, with no offsetting criticism in the same paragraph.
0
Mixed or unclear.
Paragraph contains both praise and concern. Or contains neither. Or only describes facts without evaluation.
−1
US criticizes the country's IP regime.
Paragraph contains explicit concern about piracy, enforcement gaps, or Watch List status, with no offsetting praise in the same paragraph.

Three sentences. Three rules. Anything beyond this is iteration. The codebook will get longer as you discover edge cases.

Day 2 · Movement 0211 / 30
Three rules for the codebook

What separates a codebook
from a wish.

Rule 01

Operational

Each rule names a feature you can find in the text. "Mentions Watch List" is operational. "Has a critical tone" is not.

Rule 02

Disjoint

No paragraph fits two rules. If your codebook says +1 for "praise" and 0 for "mixed," then "praise with one concern" must resolve to one of the two and not float.

Rule 03

Testable

Two coders applying the rules to the same paragraph reach the same answer most of the time. If they do not, the codebook has to be rewritten.

Operational, disjoint, testable. Spend longer on the codebook than on the coding. Bad codebooks compound.

Day 2 · Movement 0213 / 30
Validation metrics

Four numbers your appendix needs.

Model versus gold set

  • Weighted F1. Class-balanced F1 across classes. The headline.
  • Accuracy. Diagonal of the confusion matrix. Always disclose, but never alone.
  • Pearson r. Continuous proxy score against the hand-coded score. Captures information the binarized prediction throws away.
  • Spearman ρ. Rank correlation. Robust to outliers and non-linear monotone shifts.

Park's published numbers are F1 = 0.787, accuracy = 0.773, Pearson r = 0.676, Spearman ρ = 0.689 under 10-fold CV.

The python call

from sklearn.metrics import (
  f1_score, accuracy_score, confusion_matrix)
from scipy.stats import pearsonr, spearmanr

# model vs hand-coded
f1  = f1_score(gold, pred, average="weighted")
acc = accuracy_score(gold, pred)
r,_ = pearsonr(score, gold)
ρ,_ = spearmanr(score, gold)

Inter-coder agreement is a separate question

If two humans coded the gold set, report Cohen's κ or Krippendorff's α in the appendix to defend the gold itself. Park's published proxy treats the hand-coded labels as the ground truth, so this number is not in her main metric stack.

A note from someone who has done this

It feels tedious.
It is the most important hour of the project.

Coding thirty paragraphs takes an afternoon. There will be a moment around paragraph fifteen when the temptation is to skim, click, and move on.

Resist that. Every ambiguous paragraph you skim is an edge case the model will fail on later. Note it in the margin. Discuss it with your co-author. Update the codebook.

Day 217 / 30
The Day 1 baseline

One hypothesis,
three labels, one prediction.

What we did yesterday

candidate_labels = [
    "the country has weak IP protection",
    "the country has strong IP protection",
    "unrelated to IP protection",
]
out = zsc(text, candidate_labels)

Each label is a hypothesis the model checks for entailment. The model picks the most-entailed label as the prediction.

Why it stops working

One hypothesis is one prompt. Israel 2017 mentioned both removal and concerns. The model picked the dominant signal.

Reasonable wordings of the same hypothesis will produce different predictions on the same paragraph. The single-hypothesis pipeline cannot use that signal.

Insight. If thirteen wordings agree, you have signal. If they disagree, you have a hard case the codebook should flag.

How the score is computed

Weighted average
of thirteen entailment probabilities.

When positive and negative hypotheses are entailed at similar strengths, the weighted sum lands near zero. That is what mixed paragraphs should look like.

Day 2 · Movement 0321 / 30
Why the ensemble works

Each wording has its own noise.
Averaging cancels it.

Mechanism 01

Lexical coverage

"Praise" alone misses paragraphs where the praise comes through "removed from list" or "acceded to treaty." Each hypothesis covers a different lexical surface.

Mechanism 02

Wording variance

Two ways of phrasing the same hypothesis produce slightly different probabilities. Their average is more stable than either one alone.

Mechanism 03

Disagreement is signal

When the thirteen hypotheses disagree, the score lands near zero. That matches the codebook's treatment of mixed paragraphs.

The ensemble does not make the model smarter. It uses the model's existing answers more carefully.

Day 2 · Movement 0322 / 30
What ensembles cannot do

The ceiling of an encoder
is still the encoder's ceiling.

What ensembling fixes

  • Single-wording brittleness.
  • Mixed-paragraph collapse to one label.
  • Lexical blind spots.
  • Calibration on the middle of the scale.

What it does not

  • Long-context reasoning across paragraphs.
  • Sarcasm, hedging, or implicature.
  • Domain shift to text the encoder never saw in pretraining.
  • Quantitative claims like "this is more critical than the prior year's paragraph."

For most political-science measurement tasks, the encoder ceiling is high enough. When it is not, Day 3 introduces the next tool.

Day 224 / 30
Aggregation

Three ways to roll
paragraph scores up to country-year.

Option 01

Mean

Average every paragraph score in the country-year. Simple. Treats all paragraphs as equally informative.

Risk. A country with one critical paragraph and forty boilerplate paragraphs gets a near-zero average.

Option 02

Length-weighted mean

Weight each paragraph by token count. Captures the intuition that long paragraphs carry more substantive content.

Risk. Verbose paragraphs are not always informative. Re-read your data before assuming this.

Option 03

Top-K aggregation

Keep only the most extreme K paragraphs per country-year. Useful when most paragraphs are administrative and only a few carry a real assessment.

Risk. K is a hyper-parameter. Validate it against the gold set.

There is no neutral aggregation. The question your paper asks decides which one fits.

Day 2 · Movement 0427 / 30
For publication

The validation appendix
your reviewer is looking for.

Must-have

  • Codebook reproduced in full.
  • Gold-set sample size and selection method.
  • Weighted F1 and accuracy between model and gold set.
  • Pearson r and Spearman ρ between continuous score and gold.
  • Confusion matrix on the gold set.
  • Model ID, version pin, hypothesis list, weights.

Often asked for

  • Subgroup performance (region, time period, length).
  • Hypothesis-level ablation. Drop one, re-run.
  • Held-out sample, re-coded after the fact.
  • Comparison against a simpler baseline (lexicon, single hypothesis).
  • The notebook and the gold-set CSV in the replication package.

If the appendix has all of column one, the proxy is defensible. Column two is where revisions go.
